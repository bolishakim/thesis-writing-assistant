# Professor's Style Guide (Pammer-Schindler, TU Graz ISDS)

These rules come directly from the thesis supervisor and take precedence over generic academic writing conventions when they conflict.

---

## Blueprint Structure

The thesis must follow this section order. Each section has a specific purpose — do not mix concerns across sections.

1. **Abstract** — Briefly give the full picture. Include 1-2 sentences that cover every section below. The abstract is a miniature version of the entire thesis.
2. **Introduction** — What is the overarching topic and research objective? Why and for whom is this interesting?
3. **Related Work** — What is already known in this field? What are different research directions, and which is interesting for this thesis?
4. **Research Questions** — Which concrete research questions are asked and answered in this thesis? State them explicitly.
5. **Artefact (optional)** — If an artefact developed by the researcher plays a central role (e.g., an app, a specific algorithm), dedicate a section to it explaining how it works and the design rationale.
6. **Methodology** — How are the research questions answered? Must include these subsections:
   - Study participants and recruitment (note if ethics approval was necessary)
   - Materials — questionnaires, interview guidelines, artefacts, measurement devices
   - Data collection
   - Data analysis
7. **Results** — Be as objective as possible in reporting results. Give readers the possibility to form their own interpretation.
8. **Discussion** — Interpret results in light of prior research.
9. **Conclusion** — Summarize; re-iterate the main results and interpretation; highlight the contribution — what is new, surprising about your work? What statements can readers take from your work (and cite it for)?
10. **References** — Use a consistent citation style. Use the best possible works as references.

## Core Style Rules

### Write Simply
The content is typically complex enough. Do not add complexity through the language. Prefer short, clear sentences over long, convoluted ones. Check for overly long sentences — especially relevant for non-native English speakers.

### Use Consistent Wording
In contrast to literary writing, academic writing should reuse the same words for the same concepts and artefacts. Do not rotate synonyms for technical terms. If you call it "the system" in one paragraph, do not switch to "the framework" or "the platform" in the next — unless they genuinely mean different things.

### Create a Narrative
Build a line of argumentation throughout each section and chapter. The reader should feel the text is going somewhere, not just listing facts. Each section should have a logical flow that advances the overall argument.

### Paragraphs Structure Single Thoughts
- Each paragraph should make a single statement or advance a single point
- A new thought = a new paragraph
- Rule of thumb: approximately 10-20 lines per paragraph
- Advanced technique: differentiate between line breaks (smaller break to next thought within the same topic) and paragraph breaks (larger break to a new topic)

## Research Community Awareness

- Research is a social activity within specific communities
- Different communities have different expectations for common ground (literature, theories), writing style, and structure
- Read and cite from exactly the community you want to publish to / belong to
- Identify blueprint papers that are as close as possible in type of research question and methodology to your work — and adopt structure from those
- Research papers are written for a specific target audience — keep this audience in mind

---

## Separation of Concerns

This is a critical principle emphasized by the professor:

- **Results and Discussion are separate.** Results present findings objectively. Discussion interprets them. Do not mix interpretation into the Results section.
- **Related Work and Introduction are separate.** Introduction motivates the work. Related Work surveys existing literature in depth.
- **Research Questions should be explicit.** Do not bury them in the Introduction — give them their own section or clearly delineated subsection.

---

## Process Recommendations

These inform how the skill should approach incremental work:

- The writing will need editing — first drafts are never perfect
- Start by writing the abstract first to establish the overall storyline
- For each section, outline the major statements first (1 statement = 1 paragraph), then flesh them out
- Consider starting with the middle sections (Research Questions, Methodology, Results) since they correspond most directly to the work done
